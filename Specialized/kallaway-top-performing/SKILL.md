# Kallaway Top Performing

Create original short-form scripts using the mechanics of strong explanatory reels. Match the structural energy, never Kallaway's identity, verbal fingerprints, or distinctive wording.

## Establish the evidence boundary

Before drafting, separate the input into:

- **Verified facts:** claims explicitly supplied or confirmed by reliable sources.
- **Interpretations:** reasonable implications that must be framed as interpretation.
- **Unknowns:** proof points, comparisons, or mechanisms that cannot be claimed.

Never promote an interpretation into a fact. Never invent a missing beat to make the story feel complete. For current products, companies, prices, science, or news, verify consequential claims with current primary sources.

## Choose one story engine

Pick the single engine best supported by the evidence:

1. **Impossible object:** a thing exists that feels physically or technically surprising.
2. **Hidden system:** reveal the mechanism behind something familiar.
3. **Consequential shift:** show how a workflow, market, or behavior changes.
4. **Extreme number:** use a defensible number to make scale tangible.
5. **Decision or tradeoff:** contrast a clear benefit with a credible cost or limitation.

Do not combine engines unless one naturally proves another. One strong promise beats several weak promises.

## Build the narrative spine

Use five escalating beats when the evidence supports them:

1. **Hook:** identify the subject and tension immediately while leaving one question open.
2. **Proof:** show the strongest concrete fact, visual, comparison, or number.
3. **Mechanism:** explain how or why it works in plain language.
4. **Turn:** reveal the surprising detail, limitation, or honest tradeoff.
5. **Consequence:** land on what changes for the viewer.

Every sentence must add a new fact, mechanism, contrast, tradeoff, consequence, or turn. Delete any sentence that merely paraphrases an earlier idea.

## Write hooks that are truly different

When three hooks are requested or included by default, use three distinct structures:

1. A concrete question or contradiction.
2. A bold factual or visual reveal.
3. A viewer consequence, decision, or credible risk.

Each hook must identify the subject in its opening sentence. Keep the central resolution out of the hook. Reject the set if two options make the same promise in different words.

## Write for speech

- Prefer direct language, contractions, and active verbs.
- Vary sentence length. Avoid both long clause chains and robotic fragments.
- Put proof beside the claim it validates.
- Give numbers context. Do not turn a number into an unsupported label, such as calling 40 watts "low power" without a comparison.
- Replace abstractions with concrete actions, objects, choices, and consequences.
- Remove throat-clearing and filler, including "that's the real story," "the takeaway is simple," and "so that's where things stand."
- Do not add a summary after the ending has already landed.

## Handle thin source material

Count the distinct usable beats before drafting.

- With five or more beats, build the full spine.
- With fewer than five, write a shorter, stronger script instead of padding to a requested runtime.
- For a sparse capability-plus-caveat premise, use four beats once each: capability, implication, limitation, balanced close.
- If the user permits commentary, note that additional evidence is needed to support a longer runtime. If they request output only, omit the note.

## End with stakes

The final line should do one of three things:

- widen the credible implication,
- compress the central tradeoff into a memorable contrast, or
- leave the viewer with a concrete choice or question.

Do not restate the opening thesis or repeat every fact.

## Default output

Unless the user asks for another format, return:

### Hooks

Three materially different hook options.

### Script

One complete camera-ready spoken script, usually 45 to 90 seconds when the evidence supports that length.

### Visual proof

One brief production note only when a specific visual materially improves credibility or comprehension.

If the user explicitly requests only a script, omit the hook and visual sections.

## Final quality gate

Silently score the draft from 0 to 2 on each dimension:

- subject clarity in the first sentence,
- strength of the open curiosity gap,
- concrete proof,
- escalation without repetition,
- factual discipline,
- natural spoken cadence,
- ordinary-viewer relevance,
- ending strength.

Revise any dimension scoring 0. Do not return the draft until it scores at least 12 out of 16 and has no factual-discipline failure.

## Avoid

- Generic openings such as "AI is changing everything."
- Flat attribution as the entire hook.
- Empty hype, unsupported superlatives, stacked adjectives, or repetitive "crazy" and "insane" language.
- Fake quotations, invented proof, unsupported comparisons, or implied expectations absent from the source.
- Side quests that weaken the dominant promise.
- Calls to follow, like, or comment unless the user requests one.
- Any implication of affiliation with or endorsement by Kallaway.
